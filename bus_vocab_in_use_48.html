Key Concepts

A: Telephone Phrasal Verbs

These verbs are essential for talking on the phone:

  • Cut off: To interrupt a telephone conversation.
  • Get through: To succeed in speaking to someone on the telephone.
  • Hang on: To wait for a short time.
  • Hang up: To end a telephone conversation and put the phone down.
  • Call back: To telephone someone again or in return.
  • Pick up: To answer a telephone call.
  • Put through: To connect by telephone.
  • Speak up: To speak louder.
  • Break up: To become inaudible over the telephone.
  • Hold on: To wait for a short time.

B: Phone, Call and Ring

Different verbs are used to describe the action of telephoning:

  • Formal: to phone, to telephone, to call, to give someone a call.
  • British English (BrE): to ring someone (up), to give someone a ring.
  • Informal BrE: to give someone a bell, to give someone a buzz.
  • American English (AmE): to call someone (up).

C: Numbers

When saying phone numbers, we say each digit individually and group them. Use a rising intonation (↗) for each group until the last one, which has a falling intonation (↘) to show you've finished.

Example: 00 44 1746 845 921

"Double oh (BrE) ↗, double four ↗, one seven four six ↗, eight four five ↗, nine two one ↘."

D: Doing Things Over the Phone

Companies use special numbers for customer interaction, often managed in call centres.

  • Helpline: For technical assistance or advice.
  • Hotline: For urgent information or to report something.
  • Information line: For general information (e.g., opening times).
  • Reservations line: To book tickets, tables, or rooms.
  • Freephone number (BrE) / Toll-free number (AmE): Free to call.
